Marie Curie

Scientist · Pioneer · Nobel Laureate

1867 – 1934

Marie Curie was a physicist and chemist who conducted pioneering research on radioactivity. She was the first woman to win a Nobel Prize, the only person to win Nobel Prizes in two different sciences, and the first woman to become a professor at the University of Paris.

Key Achievements

  • First woman to win a Nobel Prize (Physics, 1903)
  • Won a second Nobel Prize in Chemistry (1911) — the only person to achieve this in two sciences
  • Discovered two elements: Polonium and Radium
  • First female professor at the University of Paris
  • Developed mobile X-ray units (called "petites Curies") during World War I

"Nothing in life is to be feared, it is only to be understood. Now is the time to understand more, so that we may fear less."

— Marie Curie

Legacy

Marie Curie's work fundamentally changed our understanding of atomic physics. Her discoveries laid the groundwork for modern nuclear science and cancer treatment via radiation therapy. She remains a symbol of what women can achieve in science despite systemic barriers — she was often excluded from scientific societies simply because of her gender. Her legacy lives on in every female scientist who followed in her footsteps.
